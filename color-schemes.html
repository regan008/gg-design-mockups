Gaia's Guide Atlas — palette study

Seven ways to color the archive

Seven complete replacements for the site's magenta-and-lavender palette. Type, borders, block shadows, spacing and structure are untouched — only the eighteen color primitives in :root change.

Each scheme is shown on the real components: nav, hero, the three "Ways to Explore" cards, buttons, and a Database listing row. Every color pairing below has been contrast-checked; ratios are printed on the chips.

Where these come from

The current palette was lifted from four scanned covers — 1983 lavender, 1987 coral, 1988 chartreuse, 1990 magenta — and then leaned hard on the magenta. But the covers were never a pink series. The 1987 edition is scarlet; the 1988 edition is acid chartreuse. Dropping pink doesn't mean leaving the source material, and three of the seven schemes below are pulled straight from it.

Two more answer things the covers don't: that this is an atlas, and that these guides traveled hand to hand as cheaply printed paperbacks. The last two come from the places themselves — a bar interior and a bookstore sign — which is a different and useful kind of source. The covers are how the guide packaged itself; the venues are what it was pointing at.

On the background

Cream was inherited from the scans rather than chosen, and it was quietly costing every scheme some snap — warm paper mutes a cold accent and muddies a warm one. So the ground is now a real variable. Below you'll find cool grey, bone white, chart-paper blue-grey, warm taupe, copy-paper neutral, warm greige, and weathered sign-green. All seven stay light — body text clears 9:1 on every one of them, and three schemes warm or cool the ink off pure black instead, which shifts the mood without touching legibility.

01

Acid Archive

The 1988 cover, taken at full strength. Acid chartreuse against black is the loudest thing in the source material and it already carries the xerox-and-staples energy of a guide that circulated outside official channels. Rust and deep teal keep it from reading as a single-note highlighter. Links run in a deepened cut of the teal rather than a dark chartreuse — the acid stays a display color, never a reading color — and a pure white joins the greys so heros and sections can alternate bands. Closest to the current site in attitude — same volume, different hue.

02

Scarlet & Bone

The 1987 cover's palette on a bone-white ground instead of its mint. Scarlet, chartreuse and teal all sharpen against a near-neutral — the mint was tinting three accents at once and softening the one that mattered. This is now the highest-contrast scheme in the set, and the closest to a poster: black bands, white field, one red doing all the shouting.

One caution

03

Atlas Blue

Not from the covers — from the thing the site actually is. Ink blue, cool chart-paper grey and signal orange is the language of survey maps, nautical charts and index atlases, and it gives the project the register of a research instrument rather than a poster. The most legible of the five and the easiest to defend to an institution or funder. Orange handles map pins and highlights, where blue can't carry emphasis.

04

Field Notes

Ochre, brick and sage on warm taupe, with the black swapped for a deep forest ink — the only scheme that changes the darkest value, and the change is felt everywhere. This is the seventies press the guides actually came out of: earth tones, uncoated stock, women's-land and back-to-the-land printing. It also takes the name literally. Gaia is the earth; nothing else in the set makes that connection.

05

Mimeograph

Near-monochrome, with all the color spent in one place. Black on near-white, safety orange as the only voice above a whisper, highlighter yellow and stamp-pad blue rationed to markers and states. The reference is the distribution method rather than the object: cheap duplication, hand-corrected, passed along. The most contemporary-feeling of the five — and the one where restraint is the whole idea, so it fails if orange creeps into everything.

06

House Red

From the bar photograph, remixed onto a light ground. What actually carries that room isn't the darkness — it's the hard-edged mural, coral cut against plum-charcoal in flat geometric planes, which is already doing what this site's borders and offset shadows do. So the plum-charcoal moves into the ink role: every rule, nav bar and block shadow is warmed off pure black, and the room shows up in the structure instead of behind the text. Coral leads, the Edison-bulb amber highlights, a deep brick anchors. Warm all the way through — the only scheme in the set with no cool accent at all.

What changed, and one call I made

Body text now sits on a light ground at 13.8:1. The coral also moved: at #e2503a it was stuck in the same dead zone as the scarlet — 3.71 on dark, 3.72 on light, failing both — so it lifted to #e8674e, which passes and lands closer to the mural's actual coral anyway. The plum stays as the neutral only: the bias in the ink and the greige, never an accent. A plum chip would walk straight back into the purple you asked me to drop.

07

Charis

From the bookstore sign — and the most on-the-nose source in the set, since Charis has been selling feminist books in Atlanta since 1974 and is exactly the kind of listing this archive holds. The palette is hand-painted signage: weathered blue-green board, gold lettering, rust and deep sky. Teal replaces black as the ink, the way a painted sign has no true black in it. Warm gold against cool green-grey is the only pairing here that feels made by hand rather than printed.

Trade-off

At 9.5:1 the teal ink is comfortably legible but it's the softest body text in the set — the borders and shadows lose the graphic bite that black gives them. That's the point of the scheme, and also its cost. If you want the hand-painted warmth without giving up the punch, keeping black as the ink and using teal only for accents is a small change from here.

Swapping one in

The token architecture already supports this cleanly. Every page declares the same eighteen primitives in a single :root block, and the semantic layer (--color-primary, --color-link, --color-accent-*) points at them — so nothing below the primitives needs to change.

  1. Replace the eighteen hex values in the :root block — but note it appears twice in each of the eight .dc.html files and once in style-guide.html, so a swap is seventeen edits, not nine. Editing only the first copy leaves a second declaration further down that may win on cascade order. Replacing every occurrence of each hex string is the safer operation.
  2. Rename the primitive families. --pink-500 holding a chartreuse is how a token system starts lying to you. Either rename per scheme, or genericize to --primary-500 / --accent-a-500 / --accent-b-500 once and never rename again.
  3. Update index.html — it hardcodes #fdfaf4 and #c8106f in its redirect styles rather than using tokens.
  4. Good news on the rest: the palette hexes appear only inside those token blocks. Nothing is hardcoded loose in the markup, including in the two 8 MB Home files — their weight is embedded assets, not stray color.

Following the pattern already set by flag-design/, each scheme can live as its own directory variant for side-by-side review before anything is chosen.
