The Dropsite Network

8 min read·Sourced from editions 8–13 (1980–1988)

Before Gaia's Guide printed a single address, it printed a promise: write here, and someone will write back. The earliest editions relied on a scattered set of dropsites — private mailboxes and forwarding addresses that let women request information without a bar, bookstore, or center ever appearing in the listing itself.1

Dropsites solved a problem the guide's editors never stated outright: many of the spaces worth knowing about had no storefront, no sign, and no fixed hours. A dropsite could be a spare room, a friend's kitchen table, or a general delivery window at the local post office. What mattered was that it was staffed by someone willing to answer mail discreetly and consistently, often for years at a stretch.2

By the 9th edition, dropsites accounted for nearly a fifth of all listings west of the Mississippi.3 Editors began cross-referencing them against bar and bookstore listings, suggesting a informal hierarchy: a dropsite was often a placeholder for a space that hadn't yet found a permanent address, or one that had lost its lease and hadn't found a new one. Tracking dropsites across editions makes that turnover visible for the first time.

The peak in the 13th edition (1988) coincides with a wave of bar closures documented elsewhere in this project.4 Read together, the chart and the map suggest dropsites functioned less as a fallback and more as an early layer of the same network the guide would eventually make visible through fixed addresses — a layer this atlas can now surface directly by pulling every dropsite listing back into the record.5
